\section{Introduction}
\quad In recent decades, advances in experimental techniques and data acquisition have enabled the recording of high-dimensional time-series data in a wide range of biological, physical, and engineered systems. Also, the network data become more accessible on the internet, which increases the research activityies of complex networks. Many interacting systems in physics, biology, and engineering can be described as network dynamics, where heterogeneous components interact through weighted and possibly directed connections while being subject to stochastic fluctuations. Understanding how network structure shapes collective dynamics remains a central problem, particularly in systems subject to stochastic fluctuations. From the physical perspective, living systems maintained by continuous exchanges of energy, matter, and information with their environment, and therefore they generally operate far from thermodynamic equilibrium, including metabolic processes and molecular motors. The nonequilibrium systems constantly consume energy and produce entropy to the surroundings, where detailed balance is broken by they directed connection topology and the noise sources of each component are heterogeneous.\par
One of the inverse problem in complex networks is to retrieve the network topology from time-series data. In most situations, the detailed connection topology is not accessible, but the dynamics of nodes can be measured. Previous studies have shown that fluctuations can encode nontrivial structural information, and that network connectivity can in some cases be inferred from noisy dynamics. These results highlight the relation between fluctuations and interactions in networked systems. However, structural inference alone does not answer a closely related and equally important question: once the parameters of a noisy network are changed, how can one predict the resulting response of the system from the properties of its original steady state? \par
To predict the response of systems to small perturbations, in statistical mechanics, linear response theory (LRT) provides a general framework for building the relation between the system's response and correlation functions measured in the unperturbed state. This connection is expressed by the fluctuation-dissipation theorem (FDT), which is valid in equilibrium systems. However, many realistic networked systems do not operate near thermodynamic equilibrium, but rather in nonequilibrium steady states (NESS). Importantly, some research has shown that the notion of linear response is still meaningful beyond equilibrium. In particular, generalized fluctuation-dissipation relations for NESS have been developed and experimentally verified in driven colloidal systems. These results indicate that the breakdown of detailed balance does not invalidate a predictive linear response discription.\par
In coupled dynamical systems, local perturbations generally do not remain local. Instead, their effects propagate through the whole network in a way that depends on path structure, and dynamical regime. This structure-dependent propagation has been demonstrated in coupled oscillators, in dynamical studies of power-grid failures. In practical terms, this means that local changes, such as node removal, link failure, or targeted attacks, can induce nonlocal effects and correlationsacross the network. Such effects are directly relevant to network disruption problems, including cascading failures in infrastructure networks, loss of resilience in ecological networks, and containment strategies in epidemic networks, where disabling a small number of critical nodes or edges may strongly alter the global dynamics.\par
A general framework that combines the linear response theory in NESSs and the perturbation propagation in networked systems is still less explored. The nonequilibrium response in relatively low-dimensional and experimentally controlled systems is verified, and some studies focus on the relation between the propagation of perturbations and the network motifs, but a unified theory that is both calculable and predictive for network observables remains underdeveloped.\par
In this work, we address this problem for noisy network dynamics near stable fixed points. Each node is driven by Gaussian white noise and interacts with other nodes through weighted directed couplings, and each of them has their intrinsic dynamics. Linearizing the dynamics around the noise-free stationary state leads to a Jacobian matrix $\bf{Q}$ and a steady-state equal-time covariance ${\bf K}_0 \equiv \langle \delta \vec{x} \delta \vec{x}^\intercal \rangle$ satisfying a Lyapunov equation. We derive explicit steady-state response functions for perturbations of intrinsic node parameters, connection weights, and noise strengths. We focus on the response of ${\bf K}_0$ and verify the response functions through numerical simulations.\par
The paper is organized as follows. In Sec. II, we introduce the noisy network model and its linearization near a stable fixed point. In Sec. III, we derive the general steady-state linear response theory. In Sec. IV, we discuss the equilibrium limit and its simplified response formulas. In Sec. V, we analyze the nonequilibrium steady-state response and compare the theoretical predictions with numerical simulations. In Sec. VI, we extend the formalism to time-dependent perturbations and discuss the corresponding dynamical response.
