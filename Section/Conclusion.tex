\section{Conclusion and Outlook}
\quad In this work, we developed a computable linear-response framework for noisy network dynamics near stable fixed points. By linearizing the stochastic dynamics around the noise-free fixed point, the problem is reduced to a Jacobian matrix ${\bf Q}$ and a steady-state covariance matrix ${\bf K}_0$ satisfying a Lyapunov equation. Within this structure, we derived explicit response formulas for perturbations of intrinsic node parameters, connection weights, and noise strengths. The central result is an operational theory for the response of the equal-time covariance matrix, which can be evaluated directly from the unperturbed network and applies to both equilibrium steady states and nonequilibrium steady states.\par

The results show that linear response remains predictive for fluctuation observables even when detailed balance is broken. In particular, the covariance response provides a direct way to quantify how local perturbations are redistributed through network-wide fluctuations and correlations. Our Langevin simulations confirmed the steady-state predictions in undirected equilibrium networks as well as in nonequilibrium networks generated by asymmetric couplings or heterogeneous noise strengths. The tests of multiple simultaneous perturbations further showed that the first-order responses remain additive within the linear regime, while the removal examples clarified the range in which the first-order theory remains informative and where higher-order effects begin to matter.\par

We also extended the framework to time-dependent perturbations and obtained explicit transient response formulas for the mean state, deterministic force, and equal-time covariance in linearized network dynamics. This extension shows that the same formalism can describe not only the difference between two steady states, but also the full relaxation process induced by a driving protocol. More broadly, the present framework provides a practical starting point for response-based analysis of noisy interacting systems from unperturbed fluctuation data. Several directions follow naturally from the present results. Since local perturbations propagate through correlations across the entire network, it will be important to clarify more systematically how the response structure depends on topology. The removal examples also show where the first-order approximation begins to break down, which points to the need for a nonlinear response theory for finite perturbations and structural changes. For nonequilibrium steady states, another natural extension is to connect the present covariance-based formalism to energetic response quantities such as dissipation and entropy production. Finally, because the current theory is built around fluctuations near stable fixed points, an important next step is to generalize the approach to oscillatory regimes such as stable limit cycles.
