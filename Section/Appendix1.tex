\section{Noisy network reconstruction formulas}
\quad Starting from Eq. (\ref{linearized_dynamics}), we have
\begin{equation}
\Delta{\dot{\vec x}} ={\bf Q}\Delta{\vec x} +{\vec \eta}(t)+{\cal O}(\Delta x ^2)\label{deltax}.
\end{equation}
For a stable noise-free fixed point, the real parts of all eigenvalues of ${\bf Q}$ must be negative. Since ${\bf Q}$ is time-independent, the linearized motion in (\ref{deltax}) can be solved explicitly as
\begin{equation}
    \Delta{\vec x}(t)=e^{ t{\bf Q}}\Delta{\vec x}(0)+\int^t_0 dt' e^{ (t-t'){\bf Q}}{\vec \eta}(t') \xrightarrow{t\to\infty}\int^t_0 dt' e^{ (t-t'){\bf Q}}{\vec \eta}(t').\label{Deltaxt}
\end{equation}
On the other hand, the deviation or fluctuation of $x_i$ about its mean value is denoted by $\delta x_i\equiv x_i- \langle x_i \rangle$, and hence $\langle \delta x_i \rangle\equiv 0$. By definition, $\langle \vec x\rangle =\vec X + \langle \Delta \vec x\rangle$. From (\ref{Deltaxt}), one has $\langle \Delta \vec x\rangle=0$ to linear order, so $\langle \vec x\rangle =\vec X +{\cal O}(\delta x ^2)$. Therefore, $\Delta{\vec x}$ and $\delta{\vec x}$ generally differ only by terms of order ${\cal O}(\delta x ^2)$.\par
Two special cases are worth noting. The first is the case of linear ${\vec{ F}}({\vec x})$ (that is, both $f_i(x_i)$ and the coupling function $h$ are linear). The resulting Langevin system is then linear, and it is straightforward to show that the steady-state average of ${\vec x}$ coincides with the stable fixed point: $\langle \vec x\rangle={\vec X}$ and hence $\delta{\vec x}=\Delta{\vec x}$. In this case, $\langle {\vec { F}}({\vec x})\rangle = {\vec { F}}(\langle{\vec x}\rangle)= {\vec { F}}({\vec X})=0$.\par
The second is the equilibrium case, such as ${\vec{ F}}({\vec x})=-\nabla U({\vec x})$ and $\boldsymbol{\sigma}=2c{\bf I}$. The equilibrium probability distribution is then Boltzmann, $\psi_{eqm}\sim e^{-U({\vec x})/c}$, which implies that the local peak of $\psi_{eqm}$ is at ${\vec X}$. More generally, for noisy network dynamics satisfying the equilibrium condition that ${\bf Q}\boldsymbol{\sigma}$ is symmetric (which implies that $\boldsymbol{\sigma}^{-1}{\bf Q}$ is also symmetric), one can construct $ U({\vec x})=-\frac{\Delta{\vec x}^\intercal\boldsymbol{\sigma}^{-1}{\bf Q}\Delta{\vec x}}{2}$, which has a minimum at ${\vec X}$.

In contrast, the interplay between nonlinearity in ${\vec{ F}}({\vec x})$ and the nonequilibrium condition can shift the local maximum of $\psi_{ss}({\vec x})$ away from ${\vec X}$. One then has $\langle{\vec x}\rangle={\vec X} + \langle \Delta \vec x\rangle$ with $\langle \Delta \vec x\rangle\sim {\cal O}(\delta x^2)$, as discussed above. Although $\delta{\vec x}$ and $\Delta{\vec x}$ generally differ by ${\cal O}(\delta x^2)$, their corresponding time-lagged correlation functions in the long-time steady-state limit agree much more closely, as shown below:
\begin{eqnarray}
    {\bf K}_\tau&\equiv& \langle \delta{\vec x}(\tau)\delta{\vec x}^\intercal(0)\rangle \\
 &=&  \langle{\vec x}(\tau){\vec x}(0)^\intercal\rangle -  \langle{\vec x}(\tau)\rangle  \langle{\vec x}^\intercal(0)\rangle \label{ktau2} \\
  &=&  {\bf C}_\tau -  \langle\Delta{\vec x}\rangle  \langle\Delta{\vec x}^\intercal\rangle\label{ktau3}
\end{eqnarray}
where $ {\bf C}_\tau\equiv \langle\Delta{\vec x}(\tau) \Delta{\vec x}^\intercal(0)\rangle$. The matrices ${\bf K}_\tau$ and ${\bf C}_\tau$ differ only by ${\cal O}(\delta x^4)$, and hence ${\bf C}_\tau$ can be well approximated by ${\bf K}_\tau$.

From the network-reconstruction perspective, ${\bf K}_\tau$ is measured experimentally or in simulations using (\ref{ktau2}) from the recorded time-series data of $x_i(t)$. On the other hand, theoretical relations connecting the correlation function ${\bf C}_\tau$ to ${\bf Q}$ and $\boldsymbol{\sigma}$ can be derived using the linearized approximation (\ref{Deltaxt}):
\begin{eqnarray}
  {\bf C}_\tau&=&e^{\tau{\bf Q}} {\bf C}_0\\
  \boldsymbol{\sigma}&=&-{\bf Q}{\bf C}_0-{\bf C}_0 {\bf Q}^\intercal.\label{QCCQ}
\end{eqnarray}
Adopting the approximation ${\bf C}_\tau \simeq {\bf K}_\tau$, one obtains the reconstruction formulas
\begin{eqnarray}
  {\bf K}_\tau&=&e^{\tau{\bf Q}} {\bf K}_0,\hbox{ and hence }  {\bf Q}=\frac{1}{\tau}\ln ( {\bf K}_\tau{\bf K}_0^{-1})\label{Ktau}\\
  \boldsymbol{\sigma}&=&-{\bf Q}{\bf K}_0-{\bf K}_0 {\bf Q}^\intercal,\label{FDRApp}
\end{eqnarray}
which constitute a reconstruction framework for general directed networks. Equation (\ref{Ktau}) can be used to reconstruct ${\bf Q}$, and (\ref{FDRApp}) is the generalized fluctuation-dissipation relation for reconstructing the noise matrix \cite{lai2026steadystate}. This relation has the form of a Lyapunov equation for ${\bf K}_0$, whose unique solution is
\begin{equation}
{\bf K}_0=\int_0^\infty dt e^{t{\bf Q}} \boldsymbol{\sigma}e^{t{\bf Q}^\intercal}.\label{Lynsoln}
\end{equation}
For the equilibrium case, or when ${ F}({\vec x})$ is linear, there is no difference between ${\bf K}_\tau$ and ${\bf C}_\tau$, so one expects more accurate network reconstruction. In addition, ${\bf Q}$ and $\boldsymbol{\sigma}$ can also be reconstructed efficiently from time-series data using stochastic force inference \cite{ChengLai2022}.


For the equilibrium case of symmetric ${\bf Q}\boldsymbol{\sigma}$, one can show easily
\begin{equation}
  \boldsymbol{\sigma}=-2   {\bf Q} {\bf K}_0.\label{FDRsym}
  \end{equation}
Notice that the right-hand sides of (\ref{FDRApp}) and (\ref{FDRsym}) are identical for the diagonal elements, so their differences appear only in the off-diagonal predictions for $\sigma_{ij}$. To distinguish them, one can plot the off-diagonal elements of ${\bf K}_0 {\bf Q}^\intercal$ versus ${\bf Q} {\bf K}_0$.

The fluctuations of the noisy network dynamics are time-reversal symmetric if and only if ${\bf K}_\tau= {\bf K}_\tau^\intercal$ for all $\tau$. One can then show that the following are equivalent conditions for time-reversal-symmetric dynamics, that is, for equilibrium:
\begin{eqnarray}
     {\bf K}_\tau&=& {\bf K}_\tau^\intercal\\
     {\bf K}_0 {\bf Q}^\intercal&=& {\bf Q} {\bf K}_0=-\frac{\boldsymbol{\sigma}}{2}\\
   {\bf Q} \boldsymbol{\sigma} &=&  \boldsymbol{\sigma}{\bf Q}^\intercal.
\end{eqnarray}
